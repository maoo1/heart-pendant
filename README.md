# LED Heart PCB Pendant

Using a CNC machine to mill a copper PCB, an LED circuit in the shape of a heart was created! This project is intended to be my submission for Creative Embedded Systems PCB pendant assignment, as well as a Valentine's day gift for my partner. If you want to create something similar, follow instructions below!

# Step 1: Beginning the Design Process

Download (if not downloaded already) and open up the software Kicad. TThis software will allow you to design and edit your circuit. My pendant was based on a 555 timer adjustable LED circuit. In order to recreate the pendant, you can download the exact same schematic I used found in the 555timerCES folder. The schematic should look like this:  

<img width="1371" height="943" alt="Screenshot 2026-02-12 142944" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/5513c9d1-36e6-46be-bb80-f502d7d741dd" />

After downloading the files, open the schematic in Kicad. This will function as your blueprint for your circuit -- showing you what parts to use and what they're connected to. Next, open up the PCB file. In here, you will see all the parts from the schematic. Following the schematic, orient your pieces in any shape you want and draw the proper circuit connections (When you click on a drill hole, Kicad should tell you what parts should be connected to what). Another thing to keep in mind is the orientation of the battery -- make sure that the part where the battery is supposed to slide in does not collide with the circuits. I attempted to make my circuit in the shape of a heart, which looks something like this:

Note: My power is not connected to anything... but please make sure everything is connected. 

For the outline of your PCB, you can either use the vector drawing tool or import any svg outline. Note that you have to specifically do this on the edge cutting layer, otherwise it will be on the same layer as your circuitry. 

# Step 2: Converting the Files

Next, we need to convert the gerber and drill files generated by KiCAD into .svg files that the CNC can read. Look to pcb_to_easel.sh and PCB_TO_EASEL_USE_GUIDE.md for the shell script and instructions respectively. 

# Step 3: Milling the PCB

This step requires access to a Carvey CNC. Connecting your laptop to the machine, create a new project and import your "simple_front" svg. This layer has all the ciruit wiring and drill pads. After, import your "simple_outline" svg, and align both layers. Looking at the final orientation, note down the x,y location of the outline, and then delete it. Set the depth to .08 mm for a good starting point. After ensuring the correct bit is in the machine, click carve. If the bit is barely skimming the surface, stop the carving and adjust the depth my a couple mm. Repeat this process until you hear a nice sound that demonstrates that you're carving at the correct depth -- for my board specifically, I used a depth of 0.14 mm. Make sure that the bit isn't going through the entire board, and repeat this process until the correct depth is found. 

Next, import your "simple_drill" svg, and align the drill holes with the pads. After they're aligned, delete the "simple_front" layer, switch the bit, and add 0.2 mm to the carving. Additionally, specifically convert the holes to drill holes, right click and click convert. They should now look like crosshairs. Now you are ready to carve. Finally, import the "simple_outline" svg again, and place it in the coordinates you previously noted down. Delete the drill hole layer, change the bit, and add tabs. Two tabs at 2.0 mm should do the trick. They allow for you to simply pop out everything when the carving is finished. After all this is done, you are ready to carve the last part!

A couple tips to ensure this process goes smoothly:
- Place the board in the bottom or top right corner, this allows for a re-do on the same board if the machine messes up.
- You can manually adjust the location by typing in the coordinates rather than using arrow keys. This is especially useful for precisely aligning the drill holes with the pads

Specific Bits:
- "simple_front" should be done with a pcb bit
- "simple_drill" should be done with a 1/32 bit
- "simple_outline" should be done with a 1/8 straight double flute straight mill

# Step 4: Soldering

The last step is to solder all the parts onto your PCB. In order to do so, pull up a picture of your KiCAD PCB file, and follow the placements of your design. Additionally, in order to calculate the frequency of your blinking, you can use the following website to play with a couple different measurements: https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/tools/555-timer-astable-circuit/

This website will help you determine the resistance, capacitor, ect. values! The following materials along with the respective values I used are listed below:
- Battery holder
- Resistor 1: 4.7 K
- Resistor 2: 68 K
- Resistor 3: 470 R
- Capacitor 1: 4.7 Microfarads
- Capacitor 2: 10 Nanofarads
- Red/Yellow LED
- Battery

A couple things to note: For capacitor 1, I used microfarads because I didn't want my pendant to continuously flash, so by opting for a higher value capacitor, it allows for the light to flash so fast that it functionally stays continuously on. These values are all subject to your own discretion. Additionally, when you're soldering, make sure the solder only stays on the pad, otherwise shorts or unwanted connections may potentially occur!

Additionally, some other tips:
- Capacitors and resistors are directionless, so you can put them in any orientation you want so long as they're in the correct drill holes.
- The LED light's negative side should be slightly flat and have a shorter wire.
- Where the 555timer has a circle is connection 1.
